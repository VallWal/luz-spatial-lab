# Product Review Board — Stress Test Readout

**Purpose:** reduce the chance of building the wrong product. Ten outsiders walked the clickable prototype (Founding Walk → Passport → Owner view) and the documentation set. Nothing was defended. Where participants contradict each other, both sides are kept.
**Format:** ten individual reviews → open workshop → synthesis → the single v1.0 answer.

---

## The Ten Reviews

### 1 · Margaret & David — retired British couple, holiday home in Tarifa

Ex-teacher and ex-engineer, 71 and 74; bought the house in 2009; there October–May, absent all summer; one break-in and one water stain ago. They don't want an app — they want *to stop worrying between phone calls to a neighbour*.
**Love:** the owner hero screen ("Everything looks good" made Margaret audibly exhale); the visit story reading like a letter from a trusted person; the Emergency Card ("give that to Paco next door — that alone is worth something").
**Confused by:** everything before the owner view — passports, walks, checkpoints are staff matters they never asked to see; the word "Operational" ("is that good? Say 'ready'"); the assumption they'll use an app at all — David asked, unprompted, *"can it just email us the report as a PDF we can print?"*
**Missing:** a phone number and a human name on every screen; simple pricing; a way to *ask* for something ("please check the shutters before the Hendersons borrow the house").
**Verdict:** would buy a modest monthly plan **if a person they've met comes with it**; would enthusiastically recommend at the bridge club. **Pay for:** peace of mind, the letter, the emergency sheet. **Refuse:** anything described as technology; any setup fee explained as "scanning".

### 2 · The Weber family — German owners, six visits a year

Both engineers, two kids, spreadsheet people. Their pain isn't fear — it's *friction*: arriving Friday 23:00 to a tripped breaker, a green pool, an empty gas bottle.
**Love:** meter tracking ("consumption anomalies — finally"); the asset registry with serials; the timeline; the honesty of "items to complete later".
**Confused by:** no way to see *when* the next visit is or to book one around their arrivals; no "pre-arrival check" concept anywhere ("the most valuable visit of the year for us isn't monthly — it's 48 hours before we land"); why the story doesn't come in German.
**Missing:** visit scheduling around their calendar; a readiness checklist ("heating on, fridge on, pool blue, beds ready"); the repair loop — "the app found a perished seal. Who fixes it? Where do I click *yes, do it, under €150*?"
**Verdict:** would buy tomorrow **if** pre-arrival readiness and approve-a-repair exist; without them it's "a beautiful diary of problems nobody fixes." **Pay for:** readiness guarantee, repair coordination, German-language stories. **Refuse:** paying extra for the 3D floor plan ("I know my house's shape").

### 3 · Alexandra — luxury villa owner, €3M property, Marbella

Owns through a company; a PA filters everything; staff of three. Buys discretion and liability coverage, not software.
**Love:** the Emergency Card's codes-never-shown posture; asset provenance ("the HVAC history alone settles arguments with my facilities guy"); the restraint of the design ("it doesn't look like software my staff would leak screenshots from").
**Confused / alarmed:** *where does the data live and who can see it?* A photographic map of a €3M home's contents, utilities, and access notes is a **security dossier** — she needs data residency, access logs, staff-scoped permissions, and a contract clause before a single photo is taken. Nobody could show her that page.
**Missing:** per-user access control; an audit trail of who viewed what; integration with *her existing staff* (LUZ as overseer of her gardener, not replacement); insurance-grade condition reports on demand.
**Verdict:** would buy the premium tier via her PA — **after** a privacy annex exists; would never recommend publicly (discretion), which the CEO should note is how this segment "recommends". **Pay for:** white-glove, privacy guarantees, insurer-ready documentation, a named human. **Refuse:** any shared/community features; being a case study.

### 4 · José — local property manager, 22 years, ~80 properties

Runs on WhatsApp, a paper diary, and being personally known at every ferretería in town. Half threatened, half shopping.
**Love (grudging):** checkpoint templates ("that's my head, in software — took me a decade"); new-hire leverage ("with this, a kid could do a passable visit in week two, and that's my biggest problem solved"); the deferral model ("finally software that admits Tuesdays exist").
**Confused by:** where the money side lives — quotes, invoices, contractor chasing is *most of his day* and none of the product; who owns the data if he adopts it and later leaves.
**Missing:** scheduling/routing, work orders, invoicing, a team view; and Android, because his three staff have €180 phones.
**Verdict:** would *use* it as a licensed platform if it does the boring parts (calendar, money) and stays out of his client relationships; would *fight* it as a competitor if LUZ operates on his patch. **Pay for:** per-property platform fee that visibly saves admin hours. **Refuse:** per-seat SaaS pricing; anything that emails *his* owners directly without him.

### 5 · Marco — independent inspector, paper + WhatsApp

Does 8–10 checks a day in season, alone. His actual pain: **the evening** — an hour of retyping notes and matching photos to houses. Sunburnt pragmatist.
**Love:** one-tap OK ("if healthy really is one tap, I'm listening"); photos auto-filed to the right room ("that's my evening back"); the bathroom scan failure handled without drama; discretion mode ("whoever designed that has met an owner").
**Confused by:** tags — who buys them, who replaces them when the cleaner peels one off, what happens at a house with none; the pips; why the walk explains things he'd learn in one property.
**Missing:** **Android** — he owns a mid-range Android and won't buy an iPhone for software; offline reliability proof ("show me it working in the garage under the house"); a way to bill *his* clients from it.
**Verdict:** would use it free-or-cheap if it provably kills the evening admin; would abandon it the first week it's slower than paper. **Pay for:** time. **Refuse:** hardware, subscriptions above ~€30/month, anything that makes him feel timed and tracked by a boss he doesn't have.

### 6 · Pilar — contractor (pool & electrical)

Gets sent to houses with an address and a vague WhatsApp voice note. Wants: what's broken, where it is, how to get in, proof she fixed it, and to get paid.
**Love:** the asset detail — brand, model, serial, photo *before she drives 40 minutes* ("right parts, first visit — that alone changes my margins"); the Emergency Card's location photos; a QR on the machine itself.
**Confused by:** why she'd need an account or an app — "send me a link that opens".
**Missing:** the work order — problem photos + access info in, her quote back, approval visible, her after-photos closing the loop and becoming part of the house's record (she immediately understood that her documented repairs make *her* look good to future clients — the contractor's incentive nobody had noticed).
**Verdict:** would use a free, link-based contractor view daily; becomes a *distribution channel* — she works for 60 owners LUZ has never met. **Pay for:** nothing directly; pays in data and loyalty. **Refuse:** subscriptions, logins, any app install.

### 7 · Henrik — property insurance assessor

Assesses water/storm/theft claims on the coast. Professionally unimpressed by dashboards.
**Love:** append-only history ("you can't quietly clean up the record before a claim — do you know how rare that is?"); dated pre-loss photo baselines ("this is the single most disputed thing in every water claim I touch"); meter trails.
**Confused by:** evidentiary rigor — are timestamps trustworthy, is location embedded, can an export be shown untampered (hashes, chain of custody)? The prototype *feels* honest; a claim needs it *provable*.
**Missing:** a certified claim package export; capture metadata standards; a policy on photo editing (answer must be: impossible).
**Verdict:** wouldn't "buy," but would *accept* LUZ documentation in claims and — the real prize — would tell underwriting that dossier-backed homes are better risks. **Pay for:** nothing; but his acceptance is worth premium discounts LUZ can sell. **Refuse:** any feature that lets anyone retouch or delete evidence.

### 8 · Carmen — real-estate agent, coastal resales

Sells second homes to foreigners; her problem is *trust velocity* — buyers who can't verify anything from abroad.
**Love:** the timeline as provenance ("a house that can prove its boiler was serviced sells over one that claims it"); asset registry for the property specification; honestly, the brand ("this would look expensive in a listing").
**Confused by:** transfer — when the house sells, does the passport move to the buyer? Whose data is it? (Nobody had a clean answer; she found the hole in ten minutes.)
**Missing:** a listing-grade **dossier export** — curated, verifiable, shareable with a buyer's lawyer; a per-transaction price for it.
**Verdict:** wouldn't subscribe; would *pull* LUZ into listings ("I'd tell every seller to get one — it's a €500 document that defends a €50,000 price difference"). **Pay for:** per-dossier, per-transaction. **Refuse:** monthly anything.

### 9 · The competitor founder

Runs a leaner home-watch operation two towns over. (His full attack is on record in the strategy off-site; his *prototype-specific* review:) **Love/copy:** the visit story and Emergency Card, immediately and shamelessly. **Confused (delighted):** the gap between the beautiful walk and the absent scheduling/money layer — "you built the violin, I run the orchestra." **Missing (for LUZ, in his favor):** speed to first value — "my customer gets a report *this week*; yours gets a mapping ceremony." **Verdict:** would not buy; would copy the visible 20% in a month; concedes the accumulated history and templates are uncopyable "if they survive long enough to accumulate."

### 10 · The sceptical investor

Seed-stage, ops-heavy portfolio, allergic to demos.
**Love:** compounding-evidence thesis; operator-first honesty; the kill lists in the docs ("teams that write these usually ship").
**Confused by:** the absence of a single price anywhere in six documents; services-vs-software revenue mix; founder dependency ("this currently scales at the speed of one trusted person"); TAM discipline (second homes *in reachable density*, not "Southern Europe").
**Missing:** unit economics (visit cost, contract price, CAC via referral, churn), evidence any stranger pays, the work-order loop where money actually flows.
**Verdict:** would not invest today; would track monthly; the milestone that changes his mind: **20 paying properties that came from referrals, not friendship, plus one insurer letter of intent.** **Pay for (fund):** density economics and the dossier standard. **Refuse:** funding AR, 3D, or "AI-first" anything.

## The Workshop — disagreements kept intact

**iOS vs Android.** Marco and José: the field labor pool holds Androids; iOS-only is a hiring tax. CTO position (from docs): capture quality and RoomPlan need iOS. *Partial resolution:* company-issued iPhones for employed inspectors defuses it **for LUZ-as-operator**; it stays a real blocker **for LUZ-as-platform**. Unresolved by design — it's the operator/platform fork wearing hardware clothes.

**Tags.** Marco (cost, peeling, "who maintains 4,000 stickers?") vs the asset-history camp (Pilar, Henrik, Carmen — identity makes the record trustworthy). Convergence nobody planned: **QR stickers first, NFC only where filth earns it** — echoing the off-site independently, which upgrades that decision's confidence.

**Privacy vs data value.** Alexandra ("my house's map is a security document") against Henrik and Carmen ("its value *is* that it's shareable evidence"). No consensus; instead a design law emerged: **sharing is always an owner-initiated, scoped, expiring act** — the dossier is *printed*, never *open*.

**Who owns the passport?** Carmen's transfer question vs José's operator-leaves question vs the Webers' instinct ("it's *our* house — our data"). The room's only unanimous vote of the day: **the memory belongs to the property's owner; operators are custodians.** This sentence has contract, architecture, and marketing consequences and existed in no document until now.

**Report depth.** The Webers want engineering detail; Margaret wants three sentences and a photo. Resolved as a product rule, not a compromise: **one memory, multiple renderings** — depth is a reader preference, not a report property.

**The passport ceremony.** The competitor mocked it, José shrugged at it, Margaret never saw it, and Pilar — unexpectedly — defended it: "the house with the tags and the binder is the house I take seriously." The ceremony may be *supplier-facing* theater as much as owner-facing. Undecided; worth a real test.

**Nobody, in two hours, mentioned the 3D scan unprompted.** When asked directly, zero of ten would pay for it. Recorded without comment.

## Top 20 Insights (by impact)

1. The buyer buys *care with proof*, not software; the app is the receipt (all ten, in different words).
2. The repair/work-order loop — finding → quote → owner approval → fixed → proof — is where money, trust, and retention live; it's the largest gap (Webers, José, Pilar, Alexandra, investor).
3. The owner's memory ownership ("custodians, not owners") is the trust position *and* the platform wedge — and it was undocumented until this board.
4. The visit story is the product's front door; passports are plumbing (Margaret, Webers, competitor, off-site concurrence).
5. Speed-to-first-story (days, not a mapping project) decides against the copycat competitor.
6. Pre-arrival readiness is a distinct, highly monetisable visit type the product doesn't know exists (Webers; Margaret's "before the Hendersons").
7. Evidence rigor (untamperable, metadata-sound, exportable) converts insurers — the cheapest expensive ally available (Henrik).
8. The per-transaction dossier is a second business hiding inside the first (Carmen, investor).
9. Contractors are unpaid distribution *if* access is link-based and their repairs burnish their own reputation (Pilar).
10. Android/iOS is not a tech choice; it's the operator-vs-platform fork surfacing early (Marco, José).
11. Privacy is a *sales feature* for the top segment, not compliance overhead (Alexandra).
12. The evening admin, not the walk, is the inspector's true pain — the pitch to field staff is "your evening back" (Marco).
13. QR-first, NFC-where-earned: two independent groups reached it; treat as decided.
14. Simple, visible pricing is absent from every artifact and every persona noticed (Margaret, investor, Webers).
15. One memory, many renderings — report depth is reader preference (Webers vs Margaret).
16. A human name and phone number on the product is a feature, arguably *the* feature, for 65+ owners (Margaret).
17. Scheduling/team/QA tooling is where an 80-property operator actually hurts (José) — adoption bait for the platform play.
18. The ceremony may sell to *suppliers and staff* even if owners never see it (Pilar's surprise defense).
19. Nobody pays for 3D; nobody even asks for it (all ten).
20. Referral is the growth engine at this trust level — the story must be forwardable as a beautiful artifact (Margaret's bridge club, Carmen's listings).

## Top 10 Product Improvements (independently multi-sourced)

1. **Work-order loop** with owner approval and after-photos (Webers, José, Pilar, Alexandra).
2. **Visit scheduling + pre-arrival readiness check** as a first-class visit type (Webers, Margaret, José).
3. **Story delivery as email/PDF** as well as app; forwardable, printable (Margaret, Carmen, Henrik).
4. **Link-based scoped access** for contractors and third parties — no accounts, expiring (Pilar, Alexandra, José).
5. **Certified evidence export** — hashes, capture metadata, no-edit guarantee (Henrik, Carmen, investor).
6. **Owner data ownership + transfer-at-sale** designed and stated (Carmen, José, Webers).
7. **Privacy controls**: access logs, role scoping, residency statement (Alexandra, Henrik, Margaret's "who sees this?").
8. **German/English/Spanish story rendering** from one memory (Webers, Carmen, Margaret's neighbour).
9. **Request-something channel** for owners ("check the shutters") feeding the visit plan (Margaret, Webers, Alexandra's PA).
10. **Field-labor reality pack**: QR-first tags, offline proof, one-tap dominance audit, Android answer for the platform era (Marco, José, competitor).

## Top 10 Business Improvements

1. Publish three prices. The absence is costing trust with every persona who buys anything.
2. Sell the founding visit as a paid "Property Baseline" (report + Emergency Card + dossier seed) — never as "onboarding".
3. Lead marketing with the visit story and "know your house is okay"; retire passport vocabulary from all owner-facing material.
4. Pre-arrival readiness as a premium add-on with a guarantee — the most willingly-paid-for item the board produced.
5. Insurer track: evidence-standards whitepaper + one pilot targeting a documented-homes discount (Henrik's route).
6. Agent channel: per-transaction dossier product, priced per event (€300–800), zero subscription.
7. Contractor network as free tier: link-access, their repairs credited — distribution and quality control in one move.
8. "Your memory is yours" as a published owner promise — differentiation the incumbent operators cannot match without rebuilding.
9. Density discipline: one coastal corridor to referral saturation before any second geography.
10. A human on every plan: named contact, phone hours — priced in, not apologised for.

## Top 10 Things NOT to Build (and why they're distractions)

1. **AR anchors / precise indoor positioning** — zero of ten valued it; pure engineering romance.
2. **3D dollhouse viewing** — unprompted mentions: zero; it demos to investors and serves no one on this board (and the sceptical investor didn't want it either).
3. **Owner-facing AI chat** — owners want to *not think*; a chatbot is homework (Margaret, Webers).
4. **Native Android app in year one** — solve with issued iPhones (operator mode); revisit only at the platform fork (Marco's need is real, the *native app* answer is premature).
5. **Smart-home/IoT integrations** — the Webers asked, everyone else's homes said no; integration surface area is a tar pit before density.
6. **Owner self-service inspections** — undercuts "a professional looked", the only promise everyone believed.
7. **Marketplace of contractors** — Pilar wants *jobs*, owners want *José's* contractors; a marketplace is a different company.
8. **Gamification/scores/streaks for inspectors** — Marco pre-rejected being "timed by software"; trust tools must not smell like surveillance.
9. **Predictive-maintenance dashboards** now — no fleet, no curves; a 2029 feature on a 2026 slide (investor's allergy confirmed).
10. **Drone/exterior capture** — nobody, nothing, nowhere.

## Biggest Remaining Assumptions (unvalidated with real customers)

Willingness-to-pay bands for the care contract, the Baseline visit, readiness checks, and the dossier · the 25-minute minimal baseline is achievable and sufficient in real houses · the 90-minute full walk is sellable at all · story-driven referral actually converts (forwarding → contracts) · inspectors sustain tagging discipline past week four · one-tap OK survives real checkpoint volumes without rubber-stamping (rubber-stamping risk itself is untested) · discretion-mode data quality equals live voice · owners open stories (read-rate) and re-open the app between visits (or is email enough — maybe the app is unnecessary for v1?) · tag hardware survives cleaners, sun, and repainting · insurers will actually credit the evidence · agents will actually request dossiers · "custodian not owner" is legally clean in Spain for cross-border owners · GDPR consent flow for tenants/guests photographed incidentally · the Webers' segment (frequent visitors) and Margaret's segment (long absences) can be served by one product without splitting it.

## Customer Interview Plan

**Segments & samples:** 8 absent owners 65+ (Margaret-type) · 8 frequent-visit families (Weber-type) · 4 HNW owners via PAs (Alexandra-type) · 6 field inspectors incl. 3 non-LUZ (Marco-type) · 4 local operators (José-type) · 5 contractors (Pilar-type) · 3 insurance professionals · 4 agents. Recruit via existing LUZ clients (bias-flagged), coastal owner Facebook/forums, and cold agent/insurer outreach.

**Methods, matched to assumption:** *WTP* — Van Westendorp on the care contract and single-price tests on Baseline (€X once) and dossier (per event), run at interview end, after value talk, never before. *Story pull* — show two anonymised visit stories (LUZ-style vs competitor's WhatsApp-style) blind; measure preference, forwarding intent ("send it to the friend with the Estepona house — may we follow up with them?" → actual referral behaviour, not claimed). *Field truth* — 6 ride-alongs with inspectors using the prototype on real properties, stopwatch on: baseline-25min claim, tag discipline under time pressure, one-tap honesty (compare OK-rate against a second inspector's independent visit — the rubber-stamp detector). *Hardware* — 3 candidate tags mounted at 5 properties now; monthly photo of each tag; that's the whole experiment. *Institutional* — structured interviews with insurers/agents around one artifact: a draft certified dossier of a real (consented) property; ask "what would make you *act* on this?" and write down the missing field they name. *Diary study* — 6 owners get weekly one-question WhatsApp prompts for 6 weeks ("did you think about the house this week? why?") to map real anxiety rhythms against visit cadence.

**Kill criteria (decided before interviews, so results can't be negotiated with):** if <30% of absent owners place the care contract's acceptable price above our cost-plus floor → reprice or die. If story-forwarding intent <25% → the referral engine assumption fails and CAC math must be redone. If inspectors' tag discipline collapses in ride-along #2 under time pressure → QR-at-doorway-only becomes the identity model. If the 25-minute baseline exceeds 45 minutes in 2 of 6 ride-alongs → the minimum passport shrinks again.

## Final Recommendation — what v1.0 actually contains (12 months)

One sentence first: **v1.0 is a care company's trust engine — visits that produce stories, problems that get fixed with approval and proof, and an emergency sheet — not a spatial platform.**

**In v1.0:**
1. **Inspector app (iOS, company devices):** visit flow — room-by-room via QR doorway codes, one-tap OK, findings with photos + optional voice memo, meters, exit review with explicit approval. The 25-minute Baseline visit (identity codes, utilities + Emergency Card, meter registration, ~15 photos) as the *first* visit type. Offline, always.
2. **The story:** template-assisted, human-edited, sent as **email + web link** (PDF-printable), in DE/EN; evidence-linked; forwardable. No owner app in v1 — the story *is* the owner product (the board's single most repeated signal).
3. **The loop:** finding → recommendation with price band → owner one-tap approval (in the web story) → contractor link-access with photos/location → after-photo closes it into the record. Even in a manual-behind-the-scenes version, *the owner experiences the loop closing.* This, not the passport, is retention.
4. **Emergency Card:** built at Baseline, shareable as scoped expiring link + printable sheet.
5. **The memory underneath:** append-only, owner-owned (stated in the contract), exportable — the dossier seed, sold per-event to the first agent who asks, hand-assembled if necessary.
6. **Ops minimum:** visit calendar list, story-review-before-send, and nothing else internal.

**Explicitly not in v1.0:** RoomPlan and all scanning (the corridor to it stays open; the tech stays in the lab), NFC (QR only), AI extraction (voice memos are stored for the corpus; a human writes the story with templates), the native owner app, the 90-minute ceremony as default (premium option only), predictive anything, platform/multi-tenant anything, Android.

**Why this is the honest version:** every reviewer who represents money — Margaret, the Webers, Alexandra, Carmen's sellers — buys something in this list, this year, with today's technology and one small team. It ships in months, not quarters; it accumulates the only moat the competitor conceded (history + density) from week one; and it keeps every ambitious thing we've designed — passport, spatial engine, AI, platform — as *earned upgrades on top of a business that already works*, instead of prerequisites in front of one that doesn't.

The impressive demo already exists. Version 1.0's job is to be the boring machine that makes ten strangers pay, refer, and renew — because everything visionary in this project's documents is affordable only from on top of that.
